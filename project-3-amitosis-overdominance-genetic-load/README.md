# Project 3: Amitosis, Overdominance, and Genetic Load under Fisher’s Geometric Model

## Overview

This project investigates how **amitosis affects adaptation under Fisher’s geometric model (FGM)** when populations evolve toward a **fixed phenotypic optimum**. The work extends the classic FGM framework to reflect key biological features of *Tetrahymena*, including **polyploidy**, **separate germline and somatic nuclei**, **amitotic division of the macronucleus (MAC)**, and the possibility of **sexual reproduction**.

The central finding explored in this repository is that, under a fixed optimum, **amitosis can generate a genetic load rather than a benefit**. Although amitotic segregation initially increases phenotypic variation and can accelerate early movement toward the optimum, it also disrupts favorable allele copy-number configurations once populations become well adapted. In this framework, that effect leads to an **amitosis load** associated with widespread **overdominance**.

This project therefore addresses an important conceptual contrast with earlier amitosis models: rather than universally facilitating adaptation, amitosis can become disadvantageous when adaptation depends on maintaining optimal intermediate allele copy numbers at high ploidy.

## Scientific Motivation

The broader motivation for this project is to understand whether the unusual asexual reproduction strategy of *Tetrahymena* can confer sexual-like benefits across different evolutionary settings. Previous modeling work showed that amitosis can reduce mutation load and accelerate adaptation under other assumptions. However, those models did not include the inherent **overdominance** and stabilizing selection structure present in FGM.

Fisher’s geometric model is a widely used framework for studying adaptation. In FGM, each individual’s phenotype is represented as a point in a multidimensional trait space, and fitness declines with distance from a single optimum. Mutations move the phenotype through that landscape and may be beneficial or deleterious depending on whether they move the phenotype toward or away from the optimum.

By extending FGM to a polyploid *Tetrahymena*-like system, this project asks:

**How does amitosis perform when adaptation requires maintaining an optimal allele composition under stabilizing selection?**

The answer highlights a previously unappreciated evolutionary tradeoff: the same segregation process that increases variation can also disrupt well-adapted genotypes.

## Project Goals

The main objectives of this repository are to:

- extend Fisher’s geometric model to a **polyploid, dual-genome Tetrahymena-like system**;
- compare adaptation under **mitosis**, **amitosis**, and **sex** toward a fixed optimum;
- evaluate the effect of different **pleiotropy regimes**;
- quantify the **genetic load generated by amitosis** during adaptation;
- test whether beneficial mutations exhibit **optimal intermediate copy numbers**;
- examine how the relative performance of reproduction strategies changes with **ploidy**.

## Core Findings

This repository centers on four related findings under the **fixed optimum** setting.

### 1. Mitosis achieves a higher long-term mean fitness than amitosis under a fixed optimum

In the fixed-optimum FGM explored here, mitotic populations continue to approach the optimum gradually, whereas amitotic populations tend to show a rapid early increase in fitness followed by a plateau at a lower level. This means that, in this framework, **amitosis achieves a lower equilibrium or long-run mean fitness than mitosis**.

### 2. Amitosis can generate a genetic load during adaptation

The lower fitness reached under amitosis is explained by what is termed here **amitosis load**. Under stabilizing selection, many mutations in FGM exhibit **overdominance**, meaning that the highest-fitness state is achieved at an intermediate copy number rather than at zero or full fixation. Amitotic segregation reshuffles allele copy numbers across generations, making it difficult to preserve those optimal intermediate configurations. As a result, once a population approaches the optimum, amitosis itself can generate a persistent load.

### 3. Overdominance is widespread in this framework

A major result explored in this project is that beneficial mutations often have a **best copy number** below the maximum ploidy. This implies that simply increasing the copy number of a beneficial allele does not always improve fitness; beyond an intermediate optimum, further amplification can overshoot the phenotypic optimum and become harmful.

### 4. Sex performs better only at relatively low ploidy in this model

Because sexual reproduction reconstructs the MAC through amplification of alleles from the diploid MIC, the resulting copy-number increase can be beneficial only when that amplification remains close to the best copy number of beneficial mutations. Under low ploidy, sex can perform slightly better than amitosis. Under high ploidy, however, allele amplification may overshoot the optimum, reducing the advantage of sex and in some cases making it less effective than amitosis.

## Methods and Approach

### Polyploid Fisher’s geometric model

This project extends classic Fisher’s geometric model to a **polyploid Tetrahymena-like system**. Each individual contains:

- a **diploid MIC**;
- an **n-ploid MAC**;
- **L loci**, with the number of loci matching the number of phenotypic dimensions.

The phenotype is determined by the MAC and represented as a point in an L-dimensional trait space. Fitness is determined by the Euclidean distance between that phenotype and a fixed optimum.

### Mutation and phenotypic effects

Mutations arise in both MAC and MIC, but only mutations in the MAC affect phenotype immediately. Mutation effects are drawn from normal or multivariate normal distributions depending on the pleiotropy setting. The simulations examine multiple assumptions, including:

- **no pleiotropy**;
- **restricted pleiotropy**;
- **universal pleiotropy**;
- and, in several notebooks, an **infinite-allele assumption**.

### Reproduction strategies

The project compares three reproduction strategies:

- **Mitosis**, in which offspring inherit an identical MAC from the parent;
- **Amitosis**, in which MAC alleles are duplicated and then randomly reassorted with copy-number control;
- **Sex**, in which haploid nuclei derived from MICs fuse and then give rise to new MIC and MAC genomes, with MAC allele copy numbers amplified from the diploid state.

These different mechanisms create different patterns of phenotypic variance and different abilities to preserve or disrupt optimal allele copy-number states.

### Quantifying amitosis load

A key methodological feature of this project is the explicit quantification of **amitosis load**. This is done by constructing populations whose mean phenotype initially matches the optimum but whose within-locus variance matches that of adapted amitotic populations. The resulting fitness loss under continued amitosis isolates the load caused by segregation and reassortment itself.

### Best copy number analysis

Another distinctive component of this project is the systematic evaluation of the **best copy number for beneficial mutations**. Rather than assuming that more copies of a beneficial mutation are always better, the simulations test mutation effects across copy numbers from 1 to n and identify the copy number that yields the highest fitness.

## Scientific Interpretation

The main conceptual contribution of this project is to show that **amitosis is not universally beneficial**. Under a fixed optimum in FGM, the increased variation generated by amitosis helps early adaptation but becomes costly once populations approach the optimum. At that point, preserving optimal intermediate copy numbers becomes more important than generating additional variance, and amitotic segregation begins to erode rather than maintain the best genotype structure.

This leads to a broader biological insight: the evolutionary value of amitosis depends strongly on the underlying fitness landscape. In a model with widespread overdominance and stabilizing selection, amitosis can create a substantial **genetic load**.

The project also reveals an important limitation of sex in polyploid systems. In this framework, sex is not automatically superior, because copy-number amplification after sexual reproduction can push beneficial alleles beyond their optimal dosage and overshoot the phenotypic optimum. This effect becomes especially pronounced at high ploidy.
