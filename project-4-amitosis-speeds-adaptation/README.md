# Project 4: Amitosis Speeds Adaptation under a Moving Optimum

## Overview

This project investigates how **amitosis affects adaptation in changing environments** by extending Fisher’s geometric model (FGM) to a **moving optimum**. Building on the fixed-optimum framework developed in the related FGM analysis, this repository examines whether the additional phenotypic variation generated by amitosis can improve tracking of an environment whose fitness optimum shifts over time.

The central result explored in this project is that, unlike the fixed-optimum setting where amitosis can generate a load through disruption of favorable intermediate copy-number states, under a **moving optimum** amitosis can become advantageous by increasing the rate at which populations respond to environmental change. In this setting, the extra variance created by amitotic segregation helps populations track a shifting target more effectively and can reduce the lag between the population mean phenotype and the optimum.

This repository documents simulation and figure-generation workflows for comparing **amitosis, mitosis, and sex** under a range of moving-optimum regimes, pleiotropy assumptions, and rates of environmental change.

## Scientific Motivation

Adaptation in natural populations often occurs in environments that are not static. When the fitness optimum moves through phenotype space, populations must continuously generate and maintain enough heritable variation to keep up. In such settings, the value of a reproductive mechanism depends not only on its ability to preserve well-adapted genotypes, but also on its ability to produce adaptive variation quickly enough to track change.

In *Tetrahymena*, the macronucleus (MAC) divides by **amitosis**, a process that randomly segregates chromosome copies while approximately maintaining copy number. Earlier work showed that this segregation process can confer benefits analogous to sex by increasing among-offspring variation. Under a fixed optimum, however, that same variance generation can become costly once a population approaches the optimum. The moving-optimum setting provides a natural counterpoint: when the target is continually shifting, disrupting the current genotype can be beneficial if it improves tracking of future conditions.

This project addresses the following question:

**Can amitosis speed adaptation by helping populations track a moving fitness optimum more effectively than mitosis?**

More broadly, the project explores how the evolutionary value of amitosis depends on the geometry and dynamics of the fitness landscape.

## Project Goals

The main objectives of this repository are to:

- extend the polyploid Fisher’s geometric model to a **moving-optimum** environment;
- compare adaptation under **mitosis**, **amitosis**, and **sex** as the optimum shifts over time;
- evaluate the effect of different **movement regimes**, including deterministic and probabilistic movement;
- assess how the benefit of amitosis depends on **rate of environmental change**, **movement distance**, **standard deviation of movement**, **ploidy**, and **pleiotropy structure**;
- quantify how amitosis affects **adaptational lag**, **mean fitness**, and **fitness variance** under sustained environmental change.

## Core Findings

This project centers on four related conclusions under the moving-optimum framework.

### 1. Amitosis can outperform mitosis when the optimum moves

When the fitness optimum shifts over time, amitotic populations can adapt more rapidly than mitotic populations because amitotic segregation generates additional heritable variation among offspring. This helps populations respond more quickly to environmental change and reduces the lag between the population mean phenotype and the moving optimum.

### 2. The benefit of amitosis depends on the rate and pattern of environmental change

The relative advantage of amitosis is not fixed. It depends on how far and how fast the optimum moves, whether movement is deterministic or stochastic, and how movement is distributed across trait dimensions. This repository includes simulations spanning multiple movement rates and dispersions to characterize when amitosis is most beneficial.

### 3. The moving-optimum setting contrasts with the fixed-optimum setting

A major conceptual point of this project is the contrast with the fixed-optimum analysis. Under a fixed optimum, amitosis can generate a load by disrupting favorable intermediate copy-number states. Under a moving optimum, however, the same variance-generating process can become advantageous because continual change reduces the value of perfect genotype preservation and increases the value of exploratory variation.

### 4. Comparisons with sex depend on ploidy and movement regime

Several notebooks extend the moving-optimum framework to include sexual reproduction. These comparisons explore whether sex provides a larger or smaller advantage than amitosis under changing environments and how that relationship varies with ploidy and optimum movement parameters.

## Relationship to the Chapter

This repository corresponds to the **moving-optimum** component of the broader Chapter 3 Fisher’s geometric model analysis. Whereas the fixed-optimum project focuses on the costs of amitosis under stabilizing selection toward a static target, this project addresses a complementary question: whether amitosis becomes beneficial again when the adaptive target changes through time.

Together, the fixed-optimum and moving-optimum analyses show that the evolutionary consequences of amitosis depend strongly on the structure of the fitness landscape. The same reproductive mechanism can impose a load in one regime and accelerate adaptation in another.

## Methods and Approach

### Polyploid Fisher’s geometric model in a changing environment

This project extends the polyploid Fisher’s geometric model developed for the fixed-optimum setting to allow the fitness optimum to move through phenotype space over time. Each population contains:

- a polyploid somatic genome whose phenotype determines fitness;
- multiple loci corresponding to phenotypic dimensions or pleiotropic contributions;
- mutation, selection, and reproduction under a Wright-Fisher-style simulation framework.

Fitness is determined by the Euclidean distance between the population phenotype and the current location of the optimum. As the optimum moves, the population must continually update its phenotypic distribution to maintain high fitness.

### Deterministic and probabilistic movement regimes

The repository includes both **deterministic** and **probabilistic** movement of the optimum. In deterministic settings, the optimum shifts according to predefined movement rules. In probabilistic settings, movement is sampled from specified distributions, often with control over movement variance along each dimension.

This allows the project to separate two related questions:

- how populations respond to a predictable directional shift;
- how they respond to noisier, stochastic environmental change.

### Pleiotropy structure

As in the related fixed-optimum work, this project considers multiple pleiotropy assumptions, including:

- **no pleiotropy**;
- **universal pleiotropy**;
- and varied pleiotropy levels in some implementations.

This makes it possible to evaluate whether the benefit of amitosis depends on how mutations map onto phenotypic dimensions.

### Reproduction strategies

The main comparisons involve:

- **Mitosis**, which preserves the parental somatic genome more faithfully;
- **Amitosis**, which introduces additional segregation-generated variance among offspring;
- **Sex**, included in selected notebooks to compare its adaptive performance under changing environments.

These mechanisms differ in how they balance genotype preservation against exploration of phenotype space.

### Fitness and lag tracking

Many notebooks monitor:

- **mean fitness** through time;
- **log mean fitness**;
- **fitness variance**;
- and the movement of the optimum itself.

These outputs allow direct evaluation of how effectively different reproductive strategies track environmental change and how quickly they recover following shifts in the adaptive target.

## Scientific Interpretation

The key conceptual contribution of this project is that **the evolutionary value of amitosis depends on whether the environment is static or changing**. Under a fixed optimum, amitosis can disrupt favorable copy-number configurations and impose a load. Under a moving optimum, however, the same segregation process can help generate the phenotypic variation needed for continual adaptation.

This highlights a broader principle in evolutionary theory: mechanisms that reduce short-term genotype fidelity are not necessarily harmful if they improve a population’s ability to respond to future change. In this project, amitosis is interpreted as such a mechanism. It may be costly in a perfectly stable environment, but advantageous in a shifting one.

The moving-optimum framework therefore provides an important complement to the fixed-optimum analysis and helps explain why a noncanonical reproductive system such as amitosis might remain evolutionarily relevant across different ecological scenarios.
